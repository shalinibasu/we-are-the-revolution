July 10th

ON THIS DAY

On July 10th, 1875, Mary McLeod Bethune was born in Mayesville, South Carolina.

Mary McLeod Bethune was an educator, stateswoman, philanthripost, humanitarian, and a civil and women's rights activist. She was born the decade after the Civil War ended and her parents were formerly enslaved. She devoted her life to ensure education and freedom for African-American citizens.

In 1904, Bethune founded the Daytona Normal and Industrial Institute for Negro Girls in Daytona, Florida. She believed that education was the key to racial advancement. Bethune was president of the school which later merged with the Cookman Institute for Men. The merged school became better known as Bethune-Cookman College and was one of the few places that African-American students could pursue a college degree.

Mary McLeod Bethune also took part in government service, advising various presidents. One of the most notibile roles in government service was with President Franklin D. Roosevelt. In 1935, Bethune became President Roosevelt's adivsor on minority affairs. Later that year, she started the National Council of Negro Women. And the following year, Bethune was appointed to director of the Division of Negro Affairs of the National Youth Administration.
